    Iceland's 'Gateway to Hell volcano ready to blow at any moment'

    The Telegraph
    By Hugh Morris 24/06/2016

    TWEET

    One of Iceland’s most active volcanos – nicknamed the “Gateway to Hell” – could erupt at any moment, a geophysics professor has warned.

    News that Hekla in south Iceland is “ready to go” will trouble British holidaymakers who recall the widespread travel disruption caused across Europe in 2010 by clouds of ash spewed into the air by another Icelandic monster, Eyjafjallajökull. More than 100,000 flights were cancelled over six days, stranding 10 million people and costing £1.15 billion in lost revenue.

    “Hekla is a dangerous volcano,” said Professor Páll Einarsson at the University of Iceland. “We could be looking at a major disaster when the next eruption begins if we are not careful.”

    Speaking to Icelandic website visir.is, Professor Einarsson advised that tourists stay away from the volcano. “There are also 20-30 planes full of passengers flying right over the top of Hekla every day,” he said. “This is a risky moment which we need to take seriously.”

    Einarsson points to the fact that Hekla has erupted regularly every ten years or so from 1970 to 2000, but has now been silent for 16 years. He also says that readings show the volcano is accumulating magma and the pressure inside is higher than it has been before previous eruptions.

    Hekla was known during the Middle Ages as the “Gateway to Hell” and is famed for having produced one of the largest volumes of lava of any volcano in the world in the last millennium, around 8km.

    There has been no official warning as yet from the Icelandic Met Office, and it is not the first time in recent years the volcano has been said to be close to blowing – travellers were warned away from the area in 2013 after an unusual cluster of micro-earthquakes around the volcano.

    Should there be an explosion and subsequent ash cloud that leads to the cancellation of flights across northern Europe, as happened in 2010, passengers will be entitled to a full refund, as protected by EU regulations.

    The April explosion of Eyjafjallajökull, also in south Iceland, has been credited in part with the country's recent tourism surge, with more and more travellers flocking to island to see huge swathes of unspoilt and other-worldly natural landscape as well as waterfalls, glaciers and volcanos.
